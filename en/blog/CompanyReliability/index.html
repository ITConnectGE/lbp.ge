How to Check if a Georgian Company is Reliable

    Verifying Registration Status Ownership Structure and Beneficial Ownership Representation Authority and Corporate Powers Check for Financial Problems Review Court Cases and Regulatory Issues Confirm That the Business Actually Operates Conclusion & Checklist

  • 1. Verifying the Company's Legal Existence and Registration Status

    The first step in assessing a Georgian company's reliability is verifying that it is legally registered and actively operating. This can be done by obtaining an extract from the Entrepreneurs Register maintained by the National Agency of Public Registry (NAPR).

    The register provides official information about the company, including its legal status, registration details, registered address, and authorised representatives. Reviewing this information helps confirm that the company exists, remains active, and is authorised to conduct business in Georgia.

    Before entering into a transaction, it is advisable to verify the following:

    • Company name and identification number;
    • Registration date;
    • Legal form of the entity;
    • Current registration status (active, in liquidation, etc.);
    • Registered address;
    • Directors and authorised representatives.

    This initial review forms the basis of further due diligence and helps identify potential issues before assessing ownership, financial standing, or legal risks.

  • 2. Examining Ownership Structure and Beneficial Ownership

    Understanding who owns and controls a company is an important part of assessing its reliability. Ownership information can be verified through the Entrepreneurs Register maintained by the National Agency of Public Registry, which contains information about shareholders, ownership interests, and changes in the company's structure.

    When reviewing ownership information, businesses should pay attention not only to the registered shareholders but also to the individuals who ultimately own or control the company. Identifying the persons behind a business helps assess its transparency and may reveal potential legal, financial, or reputational risks.

    Before entering into a commercial relationship, it is advisable to review:

    • Shareholders and their ownership percentages;
    • Beneficial owners, where disclosed;
    • Recent changes in ownership;
    • Relationships between affiliated companies within the corporate group;
    • The overall transparency of the ownership structure.

    The following circumstances may justify additional due diligence:

    • Frequent changes in shareholders or ownership interests;
    • Unusually complex ownership structures;
    • Multiple layers of companies across different jurisdictions;
    • Difficulties identifying the individuals who ultimately control the business.

    A transparent and stable ownership structure does not guarantee reliability, but it can provide greater confidence when assessing a potential business partner.

  • 3. Verifying Representation Authority and Corporate Powers

    Even if a company is properly registered and has a transparent ownership structure, it is important to verify that the person negotiating and signing the transaction has the authority to act on the company's behalf.

    In most cases, information regarding the company's directors and authorised representatives can be verified through an extract from the Entrepreneurs Register. However, depending on the nature and value of the transaction, additional documents may also be required.

    Before signing an agreement, businesses should verify:

    • Whether the signatory is registered as a director or authorised representative;
    • Whether a valid power of attorney has been issued, if applicable;
    • Whether additional corporate approvals are required for the transaction;
    • Whether the signatory's authority covers the specific transaction being concluded.

    For significant transactions, it is often advisable to request supporting documents, such as:

    • An extract from the Entrepreneurs Register;
    • A power of attorney;
    • A shareholder resolution;
    • A board or management resolution, where applicable.

    Verifying representation authority at an early stage can help prevent disputes regarding the validity of agreements and ensure that contractual obligations are legally binding on the company.

  • Step 4: Check for Financial Problems

    A company may be properly registered and managed but still face financial difficulties that could affect its ability to perform contractual obligations. For this reason, businesses should assess the company's financial and legal standing before entering into a commercial relationship.

    Key areas to review include:

    • Registered encumbrances and restrictions affecting the company or its ownership interests;
    • Outstanding tax liabilities or tax enforcement measures;
    • Ongoing insolvency, rehabilitation, or bankruptcy proceedings;
    • Enforcement proceedings initiated by creditors.

    Particular attention should be paid to the following warning signs:

    • Asset seizures or restrictions imposed by courts or public authorities;
    • Significant unpaid tax obligations;
    • Pending insolvency or bankruptcy proceedings;
    • Repeated enforcement actions relating to unpaid debts.

    While the existence of one of these factors does not automatically mean that a company is unreliable, it may indicate increased financial risk and justify additional due diligence before concluding a transaction.

    By reviewing financial risks at an early stage, businesses can better assess a company's ability to meet its obligations and reduce the likelihood of future disputes or collection issues.

  • Step 5: Review Court Cases and Regulatory Issues

    A company's litigation history and regulatory record can provide valuable insight into its business practices, compliance culture, and overall reliability.

    Before entering into a commercial relationship, it is advisable to review whether the company has been involved in:

    • Debt collection disputes;
    • Breach of contract claims;
    • Employment disputes;
    • Tax-related litigation;
    • Insolvency proceedings;
    • Enforcement proceedings.

    In addition to court cases, businesses should consider whether the company has been subject to regulatory investigations, administrative sanctions, or monetary penalties imposed by competent authorities.

    The following circumstances may justify additional scrutiny:

    • Multiple claims relating to unpaid debts;
    • Repeated breach of contract disputes;
    • Frequent regulatory violations or penalties;
    • Licence suspensions or other significant enforcement measures;
    • A recurring pattern of similar legal disputes.

    The existence of a single dispute or regulatory issue does not necessarily indicate that a company is unreliable. However, repeated legal or regulatory problems may suggest weaknesses in the company's financial management, compliance practices, or business operations and should be considered as part of the overall risk assessment.

  • Step 6: Confirm That the Business Actually Operates

    A company may be properly registered, have a transparent ownership structure, and show no significant legal or financial concerns, yet still present commercial risks if it lacks genuine business activity. For this reason, the final step of a reliability assessment should focus on verifying whether the company is actively operating and capable of fulfilling its contractual obligations.

    Before entering into a transaction, businesses should consider reviewing:

    • The company's physical presence, including its registered office and business premises;
    • Its website, contact information, and other publicly available business details;
    • Publicly available financial statements, where applicable;
    • Information regarding completed projects, clients, or business partners;
    • Market presence, industry recognition, and commercial track record.

    The following circumstances may justify additional due diligence:

    • No identifiable business premises or contact information;
    • Limited or inconsistent information regarding business activities;
    • Claims of extensive experience without objective evidence;
    • A lack of publicly available information regarding projects, clients, or operations.

    For significant transactions, businesses may also consider requesting references, verifying key commercial relationships, or conducting independent background checks.

    While corporate records and legal compliance provide important information about a company's status, reviewing its actual business operations helps determine whether it possesses the operational capacity, experience, and credibility necessary to perform its obligations in practice.

  • Conclusion

    Assessing the reliability of a Georgian company requires more than simply confirming that it is registered. A thorough review should include verification of the company's ownership structure, representation authority, financial standing, litigation history, regulatory record, and actual business operations.

    Georgia provides access to a range of public sources that allow businesses to conduct such checks before entering into a transaction. By carrying out basic due diligence at an early stage, investors, lenders, suppliers, and other counterparties can identify potential risks, make informed decisions, and reduce the likelihood of future disputes.

    Quick Reliability Checklist

    Before doing business with a Georgian company, verify:

    • ✓ The company is properly registered and active;
    • ✓ The ownership structure and beneficial owners are transparent;
    • ✓ The signatory has authority to act on behalf of the company;
    • ✓ There are no significant financial, insolvency, or enforcement risks;
    • ✓ The company does not have a concerning litigation or regulatory history;
    • ✓ The business has genuine commercial operations and an established market presence.
