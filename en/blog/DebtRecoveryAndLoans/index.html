How Foreign Businesses in Georgia Can Recover Debts and Obtain Business Loans

Eligibility of Foreign Businesses 3.2. Documentation and Due Diligence 3.3. Security Structures 4. Key Considerations

  • 1. Introduction

    Foreign businesses operating in Georgia commonly face two practical challenges: recovering unpaid debts from local counterparties and obtaining financing from Georgian commercial banks. While Georgia offers a business-friendly legal and regulatory environment, the successful resolution of either issue depends on proper planning, adequate documentation, and an understanding of the applicable legal and commercial framework.

    In the context of debt recovery, creditors should consider not only the existence of a valid claim but also the debtor's financial position, available assets, and the mechanisms available for enforcement. Similarly, businesses seeking financing should be prepared to meet the requirements typically applied by commercial lenders, including financial assessment, documentation review, and the provision of adequate security.

    This article provides a practical overview of the key considerations relevant to foreign businesses seeking to recover debts or obtain business loans in Georgia, with a particular focus on the legal tools, enforcement mechanisms, and financing requirements most frequently encountered in practice.

  • 2. Debt Recovery in Georgia

  • 2.1. Available Legal Remedies for Creditors

    Contractual Claims

    The most common debt recovery claims arise from unpaid invoices, supply agreements, service contracts, loan agreements and lease obligations.

    Additional Recovery Instruments

    Creditors may also rely on debt acknowledgements, settlement agreements, guarantees and other documents confirming the debtor's obligation.

    Litigation and Arbitration

    Where an arbitration agreement exists, disputes may be referred to arbitration. Otherwise, claims are generally resolved before Georgian courts.

    Choosing the Appropriate Strategy

    The optimal recovery strategy depends on the available evidence, security arrangements and the debtor's financial position.

  • 2.2 Court Proceedings and Interim Measures

    Where voluntary payment cannot be secured, a creditor may initiate court proceedings before the competent Georgian court. In commercial disputes, the claimant will typically be required to submit the underlying agreement, invoices, acceptance certificates, payment records, correspondence, or other evidence demonstrating the existence and amount of the debt.

    Before filing a claim, creditors should verify whether the debtor possesses assets against which a future judgment can be enforced. Obtaining a favorable judgment may have limited practical value if the debtor has no identifiable assets or has transferred them to third parties.

    One of the most effective tools available to creditors is the request for interim measures. Under the Civil Procedure Code of Georgia, the court may order the seizure of the debtor's bank accounts, movable property, immovable property, or other assets where there is a risk that enforcement of a future judgment may become impossible or significantly more difficult.

    In practice, creditors frequently request the freezing of bank accounts at the outset of proceedings, particularly where there is evidence that the debtor is avoiding payment, disposing of assets, or experiencing financial difficulties. However, the applicant must substantiate the necessity of such measures, as Georgian courts generally require evidence demonstrating a genuine risk to future enforcement.

    For foreign businesses, interim measures are often as important as the underlying claim itself. Securing assets at an early stage may substantially improve the prospects of successful recovery once a final judgment is obtained.

  • 2.3 Enforcement of Judgments and Arbitral Awards

    Filing a Claim

    Where voluntary payment cannot be secured, creditors may initiate court proceedings and submit the relevant contractual and financial evidence.

    Asset Verification

    Before filing a claim, creditors should assess whether the debtor possesses assets against which a future judgment can be enforced.

    Interim Measures

    Georgian courts may order the seizure of bank accounts, real estate or other assets where there is a risk that enforcement may become difficult or impossible.

    Practical Importance

    For foreign businesses, interim measures often play a decisive role in securing successful recovery.

  • 3. Obtaining Business Loans in Georgia

  • 3.1. Eligibility of Foreign Businesses for Business Financing

    Georgian legislation does not prohibit foreign investors, foreign-owned companies, or branches of foreign legal entities registered in Georgia from obtaining financing from Georgian commercial banks. Accordingly, foreign businesses generally enjoy access to the same lending market as domestic enterprises, subject to compliance with applicable legal and regulatory requirements.

    Commercial lending activities in Georgia are regulated by the National Bank of Georgia and governed by the Law of Georgia on the Activities of Commercial Banks. While the legal framework permits commercial banks to extend credit to foreign-owned businesses, the granting of a loan remains a commercial decision of the relevant financial institution and is typically based on the borrower's financial standing, repayment capacity, available security, and overall risk profile.

    From a practical perspective, foreign ownership alone is generally not considered an obstacle to obtaining financing. However, lenders may apply enhanced scrutiny where the borrower has a complex ownership structure, limited operating history, cross-border sources of income, or shareholders located in multiple jurisdictions. Consequently, foreign businesses should be prepared to provide comprehensive information regarding their corporate structure, business activities, and financial position during the lending process.

    As a result, the principal challenges faced by foreign borrowers in Georgia are typically related to regulatory compliance, documentation requirements, and credit assessment rather than restrictions on foreign participation in the banking sector.

  • 3.2 Documentation and Due Diligence Requirements

    Although Georgian law does not restrict foreign-owned businesses from obtaining financing, commercial banks are required to conduct customer due diligence and assess the borrower's overall risk profile before approving a loan.

    As part of the financing process, foreign businesses should generally be prepared to provide the following:

    • Corporate registration documents and constitutional documents;
    • Information on shareholders and ultimate beneficial owners;
    • Financial statements and other evidence of financial standing;
    • Identification documents of directors, authorised representatives and key shareholders;
    • Information regarding the financed project, transaction or business activity;
    • Documents demonstrating the source of funds, where requested by the lender;
    • Information concerning affiliated companies within the corporate group, if applicable.

    Foreign corporate documents may need to be apostilled or legalised and accompanied by certified Georgian translations, depending on their country of origin and the requirements of the relevant bank.

    From a practical perspective, businesses that prepare the above documentation in advance are generally able to complete the bank's review process more efficiently and avoid unnecessary delays during the financing transaction.

  • 3.3. Security Structures Commonly Used in Georgia

    For foreign-owned businesses, lenders may require security not only from the borrowing entity itself but also from shareholders, parent companies, or affiliated group entities. This is particularly common where the borrower has limited assets in Georgia, a short operating history, or relies primarily on foreign revenue streams. In such cases, banks often seek additional comfort through corporate guarantees or cross-collateralization arrangements within the corporate group.

    From a risk management perspective, borrowers should pay particular attention to the scope of secured obligations, enforcement rights, and events of default contained in financing documentation. Georgian security agreements frequently secure not only principal repayment obligations but also accrued interest, default interest, fees, enforcement costs, and other ancillary liabilities. Consequently, the economic impact of enforcement may extend beyond the principal amount originally borrowed.

    In practice, foreign investors frequently focus on loan pricing while devoting comparatively little attention to the security package. However, the structure, value, and enforceability of collateral often play a more significant role in the lender's credit decision than the borrower's ownership structure or nationality. Accordingly, businesses seeking financing in Georgia should assess potential security arrangements at an early stage of the transaction and ensure that the proposed collateral is capable of satisfying the lender's requirements.

  • 4. Key Considerations for Foreign Businesses

    While Georgian law provides effective mechanisms for both debt recovery and business financing, foreign investors should address several issues before entering the market.

    First, creditors should evaluate enforcement risks before extending credit rather than after a payment default occurs. In practice, the existence of a valid claim does not necessarily guarantee successful recovery. The debtor's asset profile, existing encumbrances, insolvency risks, and overall financial condition may ultimately determine whether a judgment can be effectively enforced.

    Second, contractual documentation should be carefully structured at the outset of the transaction. Particular attention should be given to dispute resolution clauses, governing law provisions, payment terms, default interest, and available security mechanisms. Properly drafted agreements may significantly reduce enforcement risks and facilitate dispute resolution.

    Third, businesses seeking financing should anticipate extensive due diligence by lenders. Georgian commercial banks are subject to regulatory obligations concerning customer identification, beneficial ownership verification, and anti-money laundering compliance. Delays in financing transactions often arise not from legal restrictions but from incomplete corporate documentation or insufficient disclosure regarding ownership and source of funds.

    Finally, foreign investors should consider obtaining local legal advice before commencing significant commercial operations, extending substantial credit, or entering into secured financing arrangements. Early legal assessment may help identify regulatory, enforcement, and transaction-related risks before they materialize into disputes.
